Automation in Construction

Volume 19, Issue 2, March 2010, Pages 183–196

Rule-based intelligence for domotic environments

  • Dario Bonino, ,
  • Fulvio Corno
  • Politecnico di Torino, Dipartimento di Automatica ed Informatica, Corso Duca degli Abruzzi 24, 10129, Torino, Italy
Accepted 30 October 2009, Available online 19 November 2009

Abstract

In the last years Home Automation systems gained new momentum, permeating many human-related environments, from homes to hospitals. The rapid evolution of such systems showed several interoperability pitfalls and a generally insufficient support for advanced user–home interaction. To tackle these emerging issues, recent research works defined the concept of Intelligent Domotic Environments (IDEs) where different automation systems, appliances and devices are integrated into a single powerful environment, capable of providing Ambient Intelligence (AmI) functionalities. IDEs represent one of the first attempts to define a framework for AmI environments based on off-the-shelf domotic systems. This paper contributes to extend IDE capabilities by supporting basic intelligence requirements through a rule-based reasoning mechanism. Starting from a formal model of IDE elements (DogOnt), rules are defined to evaluate environment properties. Property checking is done both off-line, for structural properties, i.e., properties involving the physical structure and configuration of the IDE, and on-line for properties dependent on current IDE states. Two rule languages, SWRL and JenaRules, are considered for rule formalization and their reasoning performance is evaluated by comparing two different rule engines, namely Jess and Jena. Results show that rule-based reasoning can deal with quite complex property checking, effectively addressing basic intelligence for IDEs and providing the basis for more advanced behaviors such as user adaptation and proactive interaction.

Keywords

  • Home automation Intelligent Domotic Environment;
  • Ambient Intelligence;
  • Rule-based reasoning;
  • Property checking

1. Introduction

Home Automation systems recently gained new momentum thanks to the increasing availability of low-cost components, and to the growing demand for more automated and advanced user interaction with surrounding environments. Currently available solutions (known as domotic systems or domotic plants) are designed as evolutions of traditional electric plants, each satisfying a different set of functional and marketing requirements. As a consequence, they suffer from two main drawbacks: a lack of interoperability due to independent development and to the absence of a global standardization effort, and a lack of intelligence beyond simple automation.

Ambient Intelligence research traditionally tackled these issues by introducing the concept of Smart Home [1], a home pervaded by sensors, actuators and equipped with dedicated hardware and software supporting intelligent behaviors. Smart homes have been actively researched since the late '90s and pursue a revolutionary approach to the home concept, from design to final deployment. However, they still retain a futuristic connotation as the involved costs are very high and the required devices are often custom built. On the converse, very recent works try to address inter-operation and intelligence issues by integrating existing domotic systems with a device (Home Gateway [2] and [3]) that allows inter-operation and provides means for designing more complex automation scenarios. In this evolutionary approach, the home evolves in a so-called Intelligent Domotic Environment (IDE).

IDEs [4] and [5] address the interoperation problem with a strong modeling component, where the home environment is represented through a formal definition, in form of ontology. Modeling is the key to abstraction, allowing to make device and appliance descriptions independent from technology-specific aspects and enabling integrated automation scenarios. If modeled information also includes structural aspects and house states, the resulting ontology can be used as a knowledge base on which building more complex intelligent behaviors. In this case, resulting IDEs promise to reach capabilities comparable to smart homes at a fraction of the cost, as the needed components (hardware and software) are already available.

This paper explores the kind of intelligent behaviors that IDEs can gain with the adoption of rule-based reasoning. Starting from a technology independent formalization of home environments based on the DogOnt [6] ontology, different types of rules are defined, with increasing complexity. Structural rules permit to check constraints on the home, and plant, structure and configuration, with respect to architectural elements, devices and appliances. State rules, instead, verify home conditions depending also on the current state of devices and appliances, providing support for more advanced policies. Structural rules are usually executed off-line, while state rules can be checked on-line or off-line depending on the application domain.

Structural and state properties are formalized by means of two state-of-the-art, ontology-aware, rule languages: the standard Semantic Web Rule Language (SWRL) [7] and the Jena rule language [8], a de-facto standard for Java-based semantic solutions. Resulting rule bases are then executed by means of an instance of the Jess reasoner [9], through the SWRL/Jess bridge, and a Jena [10] instance for Jena Rules. The reasoning performance of the engines is evaluated with respect to 2 different benchmarks: a small domestic environment, composed of 6 rooms, and a more complex office environment corresponding to the university department in which the authors work (52 rooms). Results show that rule-based reasoning can be effectively applied to evaluate IDE properties, providing the basis for building on-line, context-aware, proactive behaviors. Besides feasibility evaluation, useful hints on rule engines performance have been derived. The Jess rule engine resulted more efficient than the Jena reasoner at the cost of a lower flexibility. Both engines showed difficulties in tackling multi-stage reasoning, i.e., in problems requiring different reasoning steps for their solution.

The paper is organized as follows: Section 2 briefly introduces the logic architecture of IDEs and how they are modeled in DogOnt, while Section 3 defines property types and provides example application domains. Section 4 introduces case studies for structural and state properties, of increasing complexity, whereas Section 5 describes the implementation of analyzed properties in Jess and Jena, and the design of a multi-stage extension for the Jena rule engine. Section 6 reports evaluation results, while Section 7 provides information about relevant related works. Eventually, Section 8 draws conclusions and proposes future investigations.

2. Basic principles

2.1. Intelligent Domotic Environments

An Intelligent Domotic Environment (IDE, Fig. 1) is usually composed by one, or more, domotic systems, by a variable set of (smart) home appliances, and by a Home Gateway that allows to implement inter-operation policies and to provide intelligent behaviors.

Domotic systems usually include domotic devices such as plugs, lights, door and shutter actuators, etc., and a so-called network-level gateway that allows to tunnel low-level protocol messages over more versatile, application independent, interconnection technologies, e.g., Ethernet. These gateways are not suitable for implementing features needed by IDEs since they have reduced computational power and they are usually closed, i.e., they cannot be programmed to provide more than factory default functionalities. However, they play a significant role in an IDE architecture as they offer an easy to exploit access point to domotic systems.

Appliances can be either “dumb” devices that can only be controlled by switching on and off the plugs to which they are connected or “smart” devices able to provide complex functionalities and to control (or be controlled by) other devices, through a specific, often IP-based, communication protocol.

The Home Gateway is the key component for achieving inter-operation and intelligence in IDEs; it is designed to respond to different requirements, ranging from simple bridging of network-specific protocols to complex interaction support. The cornerstone of intelligent behaviors and applications is a suitable house modeling methodology and language.

In this paper, we consider IDEs based on the DOG [4] gateway, which exploits a DogOnt ontology [6] instance to address the knowledge representation problem.

2.2. DogOnt

The DOG gateway models the house structure and the installed domotic components, together with their states and functionalities, by adopting a domain-specific ontology, called DogOnt [6]. According to the classical Gruber's definition [11] an ontology is an “explicit specification of a conceptualization,” which is, in turn, “the objects, concepts, and other entities that are presumed to exist in some area of interest and the relationships that hold among them”. W3C Semantic Web standard suggests a specific formalism for encoding ontologies (OWL), in three variants that provide different expressive power [12].
